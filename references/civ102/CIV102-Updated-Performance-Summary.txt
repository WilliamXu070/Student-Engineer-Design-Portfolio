Updated CIV102 Performance Summary

Project:
The Bifrost / CIV102 Matboard Bridge

Observed Final Performance:
- Actual failure load: 580 N
- Predicted final failure load from report: 1367 N
- Prediction realization: 42.4% of predicted capacity
- Final bridge mass: 704 g
- Self-weight: 6.91 N
- Load per gram of bridge mass: 0.824 N/g
- Strength-to-self-weight ratio: 83.98

Observed Failure Mode:
- The bridge failed at the front splice rather than through the analytically predicted global compression mode.
- The splice appears to have been placed slightly off-center during manufacturing and assembly.
- Up until the observed splice failure, there were no clear visible signs of progressive failure elsewhere in the structure.
- The failure therefore appears to have been dominated by manufacturing uncertainty and splice placement error rather than by the idealized structural behavior assumed in the final analytical model.

Evidence In Context Folder:
- Splice Failure.jpg: highlights the front splice region where failure initiated.
- failure.png: captures the bridge during / immediately after failure under testing load.

Interpretation:
- The final bridge was analytically strong on paper, but the observed performance shows that splice reliability became the real governing weakness in the physical artifact.
- This means the design was more sensitive to fabrication uncertainty than the final model captured.
- In hindsight, the splice strategy introduced a local failure risk that overwhelmed the expected global failure mode.

Design Lesson:
- A bridge can be structurally optimized in analysis while still underperforming if its local manufacturing details are too fragile or too sensitive to placement error.
- For this project, the splice design and splice placement method should have been treated as a primary engineering decision, not a secondary construction detail.

Future Improvement Direction:
- Redesign the splice strategy so failure is less sensitive to one long, misaligned splice.
- Replace long splice regions with multiple smaller, more frequent splices distributed lower in the structure.
- Increase redundancy so that local manufacturing error at one splice does not trigger immediate catastrophic failure.
- Incorporate splice placement tolerance and fabrication uncertainty directly into future design iterations and verification logic.
